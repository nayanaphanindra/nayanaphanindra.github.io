Most gamification in corporate learning is just a leaderboard slapped onto a compliance module. Points for completing slides. Badges for watching a video. It looks like engagement — but it doesn't produce it.

Here's what's actually going on, and what works instead.

## The Octalysis Framework Changed How I Think About This

Yu-kai Chou's Octalysis framework breaks motivation into eight core drives — from **Meaning & Calling** (the feeling that what you're doing matters) to **Loss & Avoidance** (not wanting to fall behind). Most corporate gamification only touches drives 6 and 8 — scarcity and loss. That's why it feels hollow.

When I design gamified learning, I ask: which drives are *actually* being activated here? If the answer is only "they might feel bad if they don't finish," I go back to the drawing board.

## What Behaviour-Driven Design Looks Like

The shift is from *rewarding completion* to *rewarding demonstrated behaviour*.

Instead of: "You watched all 5 modules → 🏅 badge."

Try: "You correctly de-escalated a difficult customer scenario → here's what you did well, and here's the nuance you missed."

The feedback loop is the reward. The learner grows. That's the game.

## Three Things I've Seen Work

1. **Branching scenarios with real consequences** — not "Wrong, try again" but "Here's what happened when you made that choice, and why."
2. **Peer cohort visibility** — not a leaderboard, but a shared progress wall that creates community accountability.
3. **Choice architecture** — letting learners choose *which* challenge level to attempt, which gives ownership without sacrificing rigour.

---

The goal of gamification is to make the *learning* compelling — not to make the *completion* look fun. When I keep that distinction clear, the design choices get much easier.
