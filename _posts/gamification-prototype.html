# Beyond the Multiple-Choice Safety Quiz: Designing for the "Real Day" **By Nayana Phanindra** --- Most compliance training is designed for the trainer's convenience, not the learner's performance. We've all seen it: a 20-slide deck followed by a five-question quiz. If the learner can recognise the correct answer written in plain English, we check a box and say they are "trained." But recognition is not the same as application. In the real world, there are no multiple-choice bubbles floating over a hazard. When I set out to build *Clear to Board*, my goal wasn't just to teach maritime regulations. It was to bridge the gap between knowing a rule and executing a judgement under pressure. ![Side-by-side comparison of a traditional multiple-choice quiz versus the Clear to Board deck inspection scene, illustrating the gap between recognising a rule and applying it under pressure.]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/quiz_vs_inspection_comparison.svg) --- ## The Performance Gap: Knowledge vs. Vision The specific challenge for a Port State Control Officer isn't just reciting SOLAS II-2 or MARPOL regulations. It's the ability to spot a violation on a noisy, crowded deck with four minutes on the clock before the ship is cleared to sail. Traditional eLearning conflates these tasks. To solve this, I anchored the design to one question: **What does the learner need to DO?** They need to observe, judge, and decide. --- ## Applying the Octalysis Framework I didn't want "gamification" for the sake of fun; I wanted it for the sake of behavioural science. I used Yu-kai Chou's Octalysis Framework to drive specific learner behaviours: - **Core Drive 6 (Scarcity & Impatience):** The 4-minute mission timer simulates the cognitive load and stress of a real inspection. If you can't find the violation under pressure, you're not yet ready for the real task. - **Core Drive 1 (Epic Meaning & Calling):** The learner isn't a "student"; they are a Port Officer ensuring vessel safety. Every "Clear" or "Flag" decision carries the weight of a real-world consequence. One design decision I want to flag explicitly: the timer is not rewarding speed. It is replicating pressure. Those are different things, and the scoring logic reflects that distinction. A learner who finishes in two minutes but misses one active violation receives an automatic Detained outcome — regardless of their other correct calls. In high-stakes compliance, partial accuracy does not offset a missed safety deficiency. The scoring model had to say that clearly, or the design would have been dishonest about what it was teaching. --- ## Architecture over Artifact: The Prototype Process I chose to hand-code this project in HTML/CSS/JS as a functional prototype. Why code from scratch instead of starting in an authoring tool? I needed to validate the consequence logic — the "brain" of the simulation. In a "Real Day, Real Job" scenario, a false alarm is often as costly as a missed violation. By building this logic-test first, I could confirm the decision-making flow before moving into Articulate Storyline 360 for final SCORM-compliant production. The HTML prototype also served a second purpose: it is the demo artefact itself. It runs in any browser, requires no LMS, no Storyline licence, no SCORM player. For a portfolio piece shown in a live conversation, that is a feature, not a workaround. ![Annotated screenshot of the Clear to Board Zone 2 deck inspection prototype, with callouts identifying the 4-minute countdown timer, progress pips, hover-click-decide hotspot interaction, and the briefing panel with Octalysis drive mapping.]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/annotated_prototype_screenshot.svg) *[View the Full Design Document & Octalysis Mapping (PDF)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1eWqngdlwOAUBupK_ra6EfjsTm0gdjITv/view?usp=sharing)* --- ## The Vision: From Snippet to System While the current demo is a standalone performance test, the full architecture is designed to lead into a "Captain's Debrief." Instead of a percentage score, learners receive a detailed breakdown of their professional judgement — mapping their hits and misses directly back to the specific regulations they'll apply on the job. This is how I approach every project: big-picture performance first, tool selection second. If the content doesn't help a learner do something differently tomorrow, it doesn't belong in the course today. --- ## Behind the Build: Designing Without SME Access Here is the part most portfolio pieces leave out. I had no maritime background and no subject matter expert to consult when I started this project. I had never been on a cargo vessel, had no working relationship with a Port State Control Officer, and could not have told you the difference between SOLAS and MARPOL before this build began. In instructional design, this is the no-SME-access challenge — you need enough subject matter credibility to design meaningfully, but no practitioner to interview, validate against, or challenge your assumptions. This is not a limitation unique to this project. It is the normal condition of much ID work. The skill is not knowing everything — it is knowing how to get to *enough*, responsibly. **Sourcing the regulations** I started with three primary sources, chosen deliberately rather than just taking the first search results. The **SafetyCulture Port State Control checklist** gave me a structured inventory of the most commonly cited deficiency categories — fire safety, lifesaving equipment, documentation, ISM compliance. This told me where violations actually cluster in practice, not just what the regulations say in theory. The **Nautilus Shipping PSC guide** gave me the inspector's workflow: documents first, then physical inspection, in that order. That sequence became the three-zone structure of the module. The **Washington State Department of Ecology cargo and passenger vessel boarding checklist** is a real government inspection document — it gave me the specific, nameable violations that make the debrief consequence statements feel credible rather than invented. All three sources are linked in the appendix below. **Where AI came in** Once I had the source documents, I used AI — specifically Claude — as a research processing tool. Maritime regulations are dense, cross-referenced, and written for legal precision rather than instructional clarity. What would have taken days of solo reading took hours with AI assistance: I fed in the source material, asked targeted questions about specific regulation clauses, and used the responses to build a violation map — each item paired with its regulation reference, its visual manifestation on a vessel, and its real-world consequence if missed. I want to be specific about what AI did and did not do in this process. It processed and synthesised content I had already identified and verified as credible. It did not source the regulations — I did that. It did not make design decisions — I did that. What it did was dramatically compress the time between "I have a pile of complex documents" and "I have a structured content map I can build from." **How I validated what AI gave me** This step matters, and I did not skip it. Every regulation reference AI surfaced — SOLAS II-2, MARPOL Annex I, STCW, MLC 2006 — I cross-checked against the original source documents before it went into the module. Every consequence statement was checked for plausibility: does a propped fire door actually constitute a single detainable deficiency, or does it require a pattern? (It is a single detainable deficiency under SOLAS II-2. I verified that.) The false alarm — Observation E, the correctly placed No Smoking sign — came directly from understanding that proximity rules exist in signage requirements, not from AI speculation. The validation step is where instructional design discipline matters most when using AI as a research tool. AI can surface content at speed; it cannot guarantee accuracy at the level of specificity that a real inspection consequence requires. The designer has to own that responsibility. **What I would do differently with real SME access** If this were a commissioned project with access to a subject matter expert and a real budget, I would replace the regulation card content with verbatim SME input, conduct at least two structured interviews to validate the violation types and consequence statements, and run a pilot with actual PSCOs to test whether the scenario feels authentic or fabricated. The design logic would not change. The fidelity of the content would be significantly higher — and that gap is something I am transparent about, both in the design document and here. Working without SME access and being honest about it is a stronger position than pretending expertise you do not have. It is also, I would argue, closer to how most of us actually work. --- ## Appendix: Source Documents The following documents were used as primary reference material during content development. All are publicly available. **1. SafetyCulture — Port State Control (PSC) Inspection Checklist** A structured checklist covering the most commonly cited deficiency categories in PSC inspections, including fire safety, lifesaving appliances, documentation, and ISM compliance. Nautilus Shipping — Understanding Port State Control (PSC)** An overview of the PSC inspection process, including the standard inspection sequence — document review followed by physical walkthrough — which formed the structural basis for the three-zone module design. Washington State Department of Ecology — Cargo and Passenger Vessel Boarding Checklist (Full Version)** A real government inspection checklist covering crew certification, STCW standards, deck condition, fire safety, environmental compliance, and emergency drill records. Used to ground the violation types and consequence statements in actual regulatory language. [https://apps.ecology.wa.gov/publications/documents/ecy05038.pdf](https://apps.ecology.wa.gov/publications/documents/ecy05038.pdf) **4. International Maritime Organization (IMO) — Port State Control Overview** The IMO's own overview of the PSC regime, covering the regulatory framework, the role of regional MoUs, and the conventions that PSC officers inspect against (SOLAS, MARPOL, STCW, MLC 2006).
