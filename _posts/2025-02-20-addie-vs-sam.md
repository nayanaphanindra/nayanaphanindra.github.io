Every instructional designer learns ADDIE first. And almost every instructional designer eventually wonders: is there something better?

The short answer is: it depends. Here's how I think about the choice.

## What ADDIE Actually Is

ADDIE stands for **Analyse → Design → Develop → Implement → Evaluate**. It's linear — you complete each phase before moving to the next.

The criticism you'll hear is that it's "waterfall" and inflexible. That's fair when it's used rigidly. But it's remarkably useful when:
- The scope is clearly defined upfront
- Subject matter experts have limited availability (you need to front-load your research)
- The client needs formal sign-off at each phase
- You're new to a subject domain and need to think before you build

## What SAM Actually Is

SAM (Successive Approximation Model) is iterative. You build a rough prototype fast, get feedback, revise, build more, get more feedback. Repeat.

It's better when:
- The client can't articulate what they want until they see *something*
- The content is complex or evolving
- You have a collaborative relationship with the SME
- Speed to first draft matters more than first-draft perfection

## The Real Decision

I've stopped asking "which model is right?" and started asking three questions instead:

1. **How well-defined is the problem?** If I'm building a course on a well-documented compliance topic, ADDIE. If I'm designing a leadership development experience where "good" is subjective, SAM.

2. **Who is my client?** Some clients are reassured by formal deliverables and phase sign-offs. Others get frustrated waiting for something tangible. Match the model to the relationship.

3. **What's the risk of getting it wrong early?** For a 30-minute e-learning module, iterating on a prototype is fine. For a 6-month blended learning programme being rolled out to 5,000 people, I want more rigour upfront.

## What I Actually Do

In practice, I use a hybrid. I do ADDIE's **Analyse** phase thoroughly — no skipping. Then I shift to SAM's iterative approach for **Design and Develop**, building quick wireframes or storyboard drafts to test direction early. For **Implement and Evaluate**, I return to ADDIE's structured checkpoints.

Neither model is a cage. They're thinking tools. The best instructional designers use them flexibly — not religiously.
