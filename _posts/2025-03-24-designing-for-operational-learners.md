Imagine you're a cargo inspector. You have 40 seconds to decide if a container gets flagged. Your training was a 60-slide PowerPoint.

This isn't a hypothetical I invented to make a point. It's the design problem I was asked to solve in a recent interview — and it made me realise I'd been designing for a very specific kind of learner my whole career.

## The learners I knew

My background is in education. K–12 classrooms, teacher training, curriculum design at NCERT level. The learners in that world chose to be there. Learning, for them, is about curiosity, growth, and making sense of the world.

Nobody fails a quiz and gets someone hurt.

That's a design context with a particular kind of forgiveness built into it. If a lesson doesn't land, you reteach it. If a module is too dense, the learner skips ahead and comes back. The stakes are real — but they're diffuse, long-term, human.

Operational training is a different problem entirely.

## What changes when the stakes are immediate

Two things hit me when I encountered this brief.

**First** — I know nothing about cargo or maritime operations. You can't design what you don't understand. That's not humility, that's just how instructional design works. The subject matter expertise has to come from somewhere before the design thinking can begin.

**Second** — getting this wrong isn't a bad course evaluation score. It's a missed flag at a checkpoint. The consequence of poor design isn't a learner who's bored or disengaged. It's a learner who makes the wrong call in a moment where there's no "try again."

These aren't new problems for an instructional designer. But the stakes attached to them felt categorically different from anything I'd designed for before.

## What I'd do differently for this brief

The approach that works for a curious, self-directed learner doesn't automatically transfer to an operational context. Here's where I'd change my process:

**Talk to the workers before touching the material.** Not the managers — the people actually doing the 40-second call. In operational training, the learner's mental model of the job is the design blueprint. How they think about the decision is more important than what the manual says about it.

**Fight to cut content, not add it.** Dense source material is the problem this brief is trying to solve — not the resource to build from uncritically. My job is to ask: what does a wrong decision in this job actually look like, and work backwards from there. Everything that doesn't inform that decision is a candidate for removal.

**Design the consequence before the interaction.** In academic and corporate learning, wrong answers are usually followed by corrective feedback and another attempt. In operational training, that model doesn't reflect reality. The design needs to make wrong choices feel like something — not punitive, but real enough to build a genuine decision-making reflex rather than pattern-matching quiz behaviour.

## The underlying shift

What this brief clarified for me is that instructional design isn't one problem — it's a family of related problems that share tools and frameworks but require different design instincts depending on context.

Designing for a curious learner means creating conditions for exploration. Designing for an operational learner means building a reflex that holds under pressure.

That cargo inspector with 40 seconds doesn't need more information. They need a decision-making reflex that training built — not one they developed despite it.

That's the difference between training that informs and training that prepares. And recognising which one you're designing is where the work actually begins.
