Pre-mission · Regulation briefing
Guided mode — regulation reference
Before you board
These are the four regulation areas you will be applying during the deck inspection. Read each one, then proceed when you are ready. You can return to this screen at any time by restarting the mission in Guided mode.
SOLAS II-2 Fire protection — doors and suppression ▶
SOLAS Chapter II-2 governs fire safety on vessels. A fire door's purpose is to contain fire and smoke within a compartment, buying time for evacuation. It must be self-closing and form a proper seal at all times. Any device — a wedge, a hook, a tied-back door — that prevents the door from closing is a detainable deficiency. Portable fire extinguishers must be serviced annually; an expired inspection tag is a maintenance deficiency. If multiple extinguishers are overdue, the PSCO should treat it as a systemic ISM failure, not an isolated item.
SOLAS III Lifesaving appliances and escape routes ▶
SOLAS Chapter III covers lifesaving appliances (life rings, immersion suits, EPIRBs, liferafts) and emergency procedures. Escape routes must be unobstructed at all times — cargo, equipment, or stores in an escape corridor create a lethal hazard during evacuation. Life rings must be present in their designated mounting brackets, within their service date, and in usable condition. A missing life ring is a recordable deficiency; multiple missing appliances become detainable. The LSA Code specifies exact requirements for each appliance type.
MARPOL I Oil pollution prevention — placards and equipment ▶
MARPOL Annex I governs the prevention of oil pollution from ships. It requires a Discharge of Oil Prohibited placard to be posted in machinery spaces and near bilge pumps. This placard is not bureaucratic — its absence signals that the crew may be unaware of, or indifferent to, their discharge obligations. On deck, MARPOL Annex I concerns are primarily documentary; the physical equipment (Oil-Water Separator, Oil Record Book) is inspected in the engine room. A missing placard on deck is a recordable deficiency; it triggers a wider inspection of environmental compliance culture.
Signage Advisory signage — proximity rules ▶
Not every missing sign is a violation. Advisory signage requirements specify proximity to the relevant hazard, not exact placement. A No Smoking sign posted within acceptable distance of a fuel vent — even if not directly adjacent — satisfies the requirement. PSCOs are trained to distinguish between a genuinely missing sign and a sign that is present but not immediately visible. Flagging a compliant sign as a deficiency wastes inspection time and undermines your credibility. Read the environment carefully before making a call.
